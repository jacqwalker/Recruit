Meetings - love them or hate them?

7 July 2017

I’d go so far to say that meetings are a waste of time.

Thomas Sewell said, “People who enjoy meetings should not be in charge of anything … the least productive people are usually the ones who are most in favour of holding meetings.”

Who is Thomas Sewell you might ask? He’s an American economist, turned social theorist, political philosopher, and author. A smart guy.

Mark Cuban, shark tank investor (the US equivalent of Dragons Den), reportedly said he doesn’t attend meetings unless someone is writing a cheque. Considering he is worth about $3.4 billion (per Wikipedia), should we not be taking heed of what he says?

Yet we don’t. We enjoy the status that running from meeting to meeting seems to give us. This badge of honour is permission to brag about how busy we are.

What we forget is meetings are the opportunity cost of not finding new customers, generating ideas, improving current processes and bar all of that, getting your job done and leaving work on time.

And let’s be honest, meetings are indispensable if you are hangover or don’t have anything to do and want to appear busy. It’s the equivalent of rushing around the office with a folder and papers.

What has brought us to this point? Has the meetings culture changed? There does appear to be a new trend – let’s call it meeting creep – where not only do the number of meetings we attend increase, the number of attendees do too.

Or are meetings being used as a tool to help distract ourselves from the realities of our lives? We hide behind the busyness armour. Time spent in meetings is time not spent doing our work so we work longer hours. This all-encompassing routine stops us having to think or make decisions.

If companies calculated the cost of meetings, they’d be staggered. It’s not just meetings (which we deem to have three or more attendees). It’s also lunch and learns, senior leader talks, learning circles and networking coffees. All of which are accepted as valuable and must-do’s, but are they?

We are not suggesting companies start monitoring where and how employees spend their time. This won’t benefit anyone. What we are suggesting is that we need to change our approach to meetings. Ours, and others time, must be viewed as what it is. A scarce and valuable resource.

Our six part meeting series will look at ways to change the meeting mind-set. We don’t expect you to agree with all we say, but we do want at the very least, to start a discussion on how to improve the meeting culture.

But in the interim, before you accept the next meeting invite, ask yourself:

  • 1. Will attending this meeting help me do my day job better?
  • 2. Will attending this meeting allow me to leave work on time to be with family, friends or doing activities which I really love and value?

Definition of meeting: 1. an assembly of people for a particular purpose, especially for formal discussion 2. a situation where two or more people meet, by chance or arrangement "he intrigued her on their first meeting" - Oxford Dictionary
